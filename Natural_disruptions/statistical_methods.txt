STATISTICAL METHODS
===================

This file explains the statistical techniques used to validate hydro-climate
indicators and evaluate predictive model performance.


INDICATOR VALIDATION
====================

The goal is to test whether each indicator can distinguish flood events (label=1)
from matched non-flood baseline samples (label=0). Two complementary tests are used.


AUC-ROC  (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve)
------------------------------------------------------------------
The ROC curve plots the True Positive Rate (TPR) against the False Positive Rate
(FPR) as a classification threshold is swept across all indicator values.

  TPR (sensitivity) = correctly identified floods / all floods
  FPR               = incorrectly flagged baselines / all baselines

AUC is the area under this curve. Interpretation:
  0.5  = no discrimination (random guessing)
  0.6  = weak
  0.7  = moderate
  0.8  = strong
  1.0  = perfect separation

AUC can be interpreted as: "if you pick one flood event and one baseline sample
at random, AUC is the probability that the indicator ranks the flood higher."

AUC is threshold-free — it evaluates the full ranking ability of an indicator
without committing to a specific cutoff value. This makes it ideal for comparing
indicators that operate on different scales (e.g. mm vs m³/s vs %).


KS Test  (Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test)
------------------------------------
A non-parametric test that measures the maximum vertical distance between the
cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) of the flood and baseline populations.

  KS statistic: value between 0 and 1. Higher = more separated distributions.
  p-value:      probability of observing this separation by chance if the two
                populations were actually identical.

The KS test makes no assumptions about the shape of the distribution (unlike
a t-test which assumes normality), which is important here since indicators
like chirps_7d_anom_pct are heavily right-skewed.

A p-value < 0.05 is conventionally significant. All indicators here have
p ≈ 0 except jrc_recurrence_pct (p = 2e-6), confirming the distributions
are statistically distinct in all cases.

The KS statistic complements AUC: AUC measures ranking ability, KS measures
distributional separation. A high KS with a low AUC would suggest the
distributions differ but overlap heavily in the middle.


INDICATOR CONSTRUCTION
======================

Z-Score (Standardised Precipitation Index, SPI)
------------------------------------------------
A z-score expresses a value in terms of standard deviations from the mean
of a reference distribution:

  z = (x - mu) / sigma

where mu and sigma are the mean and standard deviation of the reference
(climatological baseline, here 1991-2020).

  z = 0    → exactly average
  z = +1   → one standard deviation above average (wetter than ~84% of years)
  z = +2   → two standard deviations above average (wetter than ~97.5% of years)
  z = -1   → one standard deviation drier than average

SPI-30 applies this transformation to 30-day accumulated rainfall. Because
rainfall distributions are skewed, the raw totals are first fitted to a
gamma distribution and then transformed to a normal distribution before
computing z — this is the standard WMO method for SPI.

Flood events show mean SPI-30 = +0.58 vs baseline mean = +0.02, confirming
anomalously wet antecedent conditions.


Percentile Rank (GloFAS discharge)
------------------------------------
The percentile rank of a value x within a reference distribution of n values:

  pct_rank = (number of reference values <= x) / n * 100

For GloFAS, the reference distribution is the monthly maximum discharge
observed at that grid cell across 1979-2019 (41 years). A percentile rank
of 95 means the discharge was higher than in 95% of historical years for
that month at that location.

Unlike z-scores, percentile ranks are bounded [0, 100] and make no
distributional assumption — they are robust to the highly non-normal
discharge distributions found in rivers.


Percentage Anomaly (CHIRPS 7-day rainfall)
-------------------------------------------
  anom_pct = (observed - climatological_mean) / climatological_mean * 100

A value of +100% means twice the climatological average; -100% means
no rainfall (lower bound since rainfall cannot be negative).

The climatological mean is computed for the same 7-day calendar window
(e.g. days 180-186 of the year) averaged across 1991-2020.

The median anomaly for flood events (+18%) vs baseline (-37%) shows that
flood events tend to occur after anomalously wet weeks, while baseline
samples more often fall in dry spells — consistent with physical intuition.


MODEL EVALUATION
================

These metrics are also used when evaluating the predictive models in
Natural_disruptions/predictive_models/.


Precision, Recall, F1
----------------------
For a binary classifier:
  Precision = TP / (TP + FP)   — of predicted floods, how many were real?
  Recall    = TP / (TP + FN)   — of real floods, how many were caught?
  F1        = 2 * (P * R) / (P + R)   — harmonic mean of precision and recall

F1 balances precision and recall. It is preferred over accuracy when classes
are imbalanced (more baselines than flood events), since a classifier that
always predicts "no flood" would have high accuracy but zero recall.


Feature Importance (Random Forest / XGBoost)
---------------------------------------------
Tree-based models assign importance scores to each feature based on how
much each feature reduces impurity (Gini or entropy) across all splits
in all trees, averaged and normalised to sum to 1.

Higher importance = the model relies more on that feature to separate
floods from non-floods. This is model-specific and should be interpreted
alongside the AUC-ROC validation results rather than independently.


Cross-Validation
-----------------
To avoid overfitting, models are evaluated using k-fold cross-validation:
the dataset is split into k equal folds; the model is trained on k-1 folds
and tested on the held-out fold, repeated k times. Performance is reported
as the mean and standard deviation across folds.

Spatial or temporal blocking is preferred over random splits here, since
flood events in the same region/year are correlated and random splitting
can inflate performance estimates.
